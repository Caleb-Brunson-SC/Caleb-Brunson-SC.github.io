Educational Approaches for Nomadic Children

April 15th, 2023

 Hello Friends! I am presenting you with an intriguing article I found entitled “Evolving approaches to educating children from nomadic communities” by Carolina Dyer. She provides three case studies from four countries which includes: Kenya, India, Afghanistan, and Indonesian. I should note that the article was published in 2016 and, therefore, the information related to Afghan children may not reflect the current situation post-Taliban takeover. Nonetheless, I will focus on the approaches taken towards educating the sea nomads of Indonesia. These sea nomads are “primarily subsistence fishers and traders of primary produce who ply their trades along the region’s extensive coastlines and islands” (Dyer, 47). I would go as far as to say that they are the maritime cousins of land-locked hunter-gatherers.



 Although these sea nomads may seem far-removed from the concrete jungles of modernity, it is crucial that they receive formal education alongside their indigenous education. This is because formal education will help them to “integrate into market economies, defend rights to resources, tackle political marginalization, and challenge policies that encourage sedentarization” (Dyer, 40). To further this goal there have been calls for Education for All (EFA) to remove the disparities in the education of nomads. Efforts by the EFA have been mainly focused on increasing the delivery methods of education along with targeting student mobility as a barrier to access. This focus on access is related to accessibility in Tomaševski’s 4A framework which falls under the Right to Education and seeks to eliminate obstacles to the education of children (Tomaševski, 12).
